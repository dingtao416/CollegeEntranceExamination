# Gaokao Chinese Essay

## Overview

Write for high score, not for vague literary atmosphere. Build a clear central claim or emotional core, make the material concrete, and control language so the essay reads like a strong exam piece rather than a template or a blog post.

Base the workflow on the recurring strengths visible in ten widely discussed high-scoring essays such as `赤兔之死`, `怀想天空`, `穿越百年的芬芳记忆`, `站在黄花岗陵园的门口`, `绿色生活`, `与你为邻`, `拒绝平庸`, `忧与爱`, `庄子的智慧`, and `老爹`. Read [references/ten-essay-notes.md](references/ten-essay-notes.md) when you need model-specific tactics.

## Workflow

### 1. Lock the prompt

Extract these items before drafting:

- The explicit task:命题、半命题、材料、关系型、任务驱动型
- The hidden test: usually价值判断, 辩证思维, 生活体验, or language control
- The sentence you must answer in one line
- The emotional temperature: cool analysis, warm memory, historical lyricism, or civic reflection

If the prompt is material-based, rewrite it into one contradiction or one relationship, for example:

- `忧与爱` -> love becomes visible through worry
- `与你为邻` -> closeness is not geography alone but shared fate and mutual recognition
- `拒绝平庸` -> refusing drift, sameness, and dead language

Do not start writing before you can say, in one sentence, what this essay ultimately proves.

### 2. Choose the winning mode

Pick one mode early and stay consistent.

#### Narrative mode

Use when the prompt rewards lived detail, family memory, school life, labor, place, or emotional growth. Borrow the discipline of `怀想天空` and `老爹`: small scene, real object, restrained feeling, late release.

#### Argumentative mode

Use when the prompt demands judgment, weighing, or response to public values. Borrow the discipline of `忧与爱`: define the relationship, then prove it with layered examples instead of slogans.

#### Cultural-reflective mode

Use when the prompt can support history, classics, philosophy, or civilizational perspective. Borrow the discipline of `庄子的智慧` and `穿越百年的芬芳记忆`: use culture to illuminate the prompt, not to show off reading.

#### Special-form mode

Use forms like letter, fable, dialogue, old-style diction, dramatic monologue, or verse only when:

- the form clearly increases force
- you can sustain it to the end
- the content would still be good even without the trick

Treat `赤兔之死` and `站在黄花岗陵园的门口` as warnings as much as inspirations: form works only when content, tone, and control all match.

## Build the essay

### Opening

Open with one of these:

- A scene that already contains the theme
- A contradiction inside the prompt
- A historically charged image
- A clean thesis sentence

Avoid a dead opening built from dictionary definitions, generic quotes, or empty patriotic thunder.

### Body

Use 3 moves:

1. Establish the core relation.
2. Deepen it through scene, example, or comparison.
3. Push it outward to person, society, history, or self-recognition.

For narrative essays:

- Anchor the essay in 2 or 3 scenes only.
- Use objects repeatedly: sickle, sky, scale, field path, old wall, letter, lamp.
- Let emotion rise through action and detail, not explanation alone.

For argumentative essays:

- Give each paragraph a job.
- Vary proof: one classic example, one contemporary observation, one inward reflection.
- Use transition sentences that advance thought, not filler like `综上所述`.

For cultural essays:

- Name fewer figures and use them better.
- Explain why the cited figure matters to the prompt.
- Land the old material in present experience or present judgment.

### Ending

Return to the image, relation, or question from the opening, but at a higher level.

Good endings usually do one of these:

- turn outward without becoming loud
- turn inward without becoming sentimental
- leave one resonant image
- compress the thesis into a memorable final sentence

Do not append moral preaching after the essay has already ended.

## Language rules

- Prefer accurate, energetic Chinese over ornate Chinese.
- Write with rhythm, but do not stack parallelism paragraph after paragraph.
- Use quotation sparingly; digest it into your own syntax.
- Keep diction matched to persona. A village memory should not sound like a philosophy dissertation.
- Keep classical allusion under control. One precise allusion beats five decorative ones.
- Use elevated language only where the essay has earned it.

## Common failure modes

- Confusing `文采` with adjective density
- Replacing thinking with quotations
- Replacing experience with moral slogans
- Writing fake profundity that never answers the prompt
- Forcing `家国情怀` into a prompt that needs concrete life
- Choosing an exotic form without the skill to sustain it
- Naming many examples but analyzing none

## Revision pass

Run this check before finalizing:

1. Can the thesis be underlined in one sentence?
2. Does every paragraph serve that thesis?
3. Is there at least one memorable concrete detail or image?
4. Is the strongest sentence actually yours, not a quotation?
5. Has the essay avoided template phrases and exam-coaching cliches?
6. Would cutting one-third of the flashy language improve clarity?
7. Does the ending feel inevitable rather than appended?

If the answer to any of `1`, `2`, or `7` is no, rebuild structure before polishing diction.

## Output modes

Match the response to the user request.

### Full essay

Draft a complete essay in Chinese with a title when useful. Keep the voice unified and exam-ready.

### Teach the essay

Give:

- a thesis
- a structure
- 2 or 3 usable paragraph ideas
- language advice tied to the prompt

### Revise a student draft

Diagnose in this order:

- off-topic risk
- weak thesis
- thin material
- structural drift
- language inflation or flatness

Then rewrite only the parts needed to lift the score band.
